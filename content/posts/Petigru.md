On Christmas Eve, 1860, South Carolina’s secession convention issued its “Declaration of the Immediate Causes Which Induce and Justify the Secession of South Carolina from the Federal Union.” Bells pealed in Charleston and, indeed, around the entire state. The Tri-Weekly Southern Guardian in Columbia declared to its readership that special Christmas prayers should give thanks for the “great blessing” which had come to South Carolina. Secessionists, and many subsequent history scholars, portrayed this historic moment as unanimous and inevitable.[^1] However, Charleston’s most respected lawyer and citizen did not rejoice. He remained in South Carolina and continued to speak against Secessionism until the end of this life in 1862.[^2]

His name was James Louis Petigru and he is, perhaps, South Carolina’s most famous Unionist.

Petigru is often remembered for his quip about his home state: that South Carolina was “too small for a republic and too large for an insane asylum.” Although the exact delivery has since been called into question, it summarizes his attitude toward what he saw as South Carolina’s insanity in choosing to leave the Union.[^3] His entire adult life was spent fighting against the rising and growing tide of disunion perpetuated by the state’s fire eaters, nullifiers and secessionists. Petigru’s dissenting voice presents the often overlooked truth that political conflict existed within South Carolina.

Often the idea of antebellum unionism is linked with abolition, Northern sympathies or political radicalism. However, South Carolina Unionists were usually conservative, legally minded and deeply loyal to southern land and society. Most were not morally opposed to the South’s system of slavery and were very often enslavers themselves. While emotionally and ideologically these men remained loyal to the South, they disagreed fundamentally with their fellow South Carolinians’ notions of nullification and secession.

Petigru’s life demonstrates this resistance.

Although he was born in what was considered the “backcountry” of South Carolina, James Louis Pettigrew (later Petigru as a demonstration of his allegiance to his mother’s Huguenot ancestry) quickly showed his intellectual capabilities and preference for academics over agriculture. He graduated at the top of his class from South Carolina College and taught school in Beaufort while preparing for his bar exam. Through this interval in his life he expanded and deepened his relationships with several lowcountry planters and their families. Among the most important relationships were his friendships with William Drayton, Daniel Elliot Huger, and Hugh Legarè all of whom would play central roles as Unionists during the Nullification Crisis.[^4]

After passing the bar, Petigru built his law practice in Charleston and quickly became known locally and nationally for his intellect, wit and courtroom skill. As the Nullification Crisis unfolded in South Carolina and John C. Calhoun’s states’ rights theories began to hold sway over its citizens, Petigru the dissenter emerged. Nothing in his background suggested that he would oppose nullification. Yet his opposition to nullification, and later succession, became the defining feature of his social and political life.

For Petigru, the United States Constitution was the only safeguard against disorder and anarchy. The Union, governed by a federal government as outlined in the Constitution, provided the framework for the legal protection of Southern, and all American, rights. The only threat Petigru perceived was the dissolution of the Union. For Petigru, nullification was not only reckless, it was unlawful.

The legal basis of Petigru’s Unionism is key to understanding him. This was no emotionally or sentimentally driven argument about Revolutionary era ideals. Nor was it, for Petigru, primarily about preserving America as a symbol of republicanism for the rest of the world. He was making a constitutional argument based on his interpretation of the legal compact between the states and the federal government. Southern interests could only be protected through correct navigation within the laws and politics of the Union, not by removing themselves from it.[^5]

But this constitutional thinking and conclusion left Petigru in an uncomfortable minority position.

While some elite white Unionists chose to leave the state after the Nullification Crisis of 1832-33 (a confrontation over federal tariff authority) fizzled out with the compromise orchestrated by Henry Clay, Daniel Webster and John C. Calhoun (sometimes called the “Great Triumvirate”), Petigru remained in Charleston even though he was never able to regain formal political status until President Zachary Taylor’s election.[^6] He continued to speak openly about his dissenting views on disunion. He continued to do business and socialize with his political adversaries. His dissent was not glaring or loud, but it was persistent and public.[^7]

Unionism in antebellum South Carolina often mirrored Petigru’s personal life: a conservative, principled refusal to bow to the majority’s will. Petigru was highly legally trained, constitutionally focused, socially well-established and intellectually independent. Because of his reasoned loyalty to the Constitution, secession, for Petigru, was not a logical progression of Southern political thought. It was a rupture.

Petigru’s story complicates the oversimplification of South Carolina’s antebellum history. Even the first state to secede did not march in lock step toward disunion. There were many dissenting voices that steadily warned its citizens of the peril of removing themselves from the Union in the nearly half a century leading up to the Civil War.[^8] While Unionists were unable to ultimately stem the tide of secession in South Carolina, they did preserve an alternative vision of Southern constitutionalism which would become central to the Reconstruction debates and postwar legal culture. Petigru’s refusal to celebrate secession, and also his refusal to abandon his home state, embody his belief that loyalty and liberty were best protected together.
