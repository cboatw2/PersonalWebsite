On Christmas Eve, 1860, South Carolina’s secession convention issued its “Declaration of the Immediate Causes Which Induce and Justify the Secession of South Carolina from the Federal Union.” Bells pealed in Charleston and, indeed, around the entire state. The *Tri-Weekly Southern Guardian* in Columbia declared to its readership that special Christmas prayers should give thanks for the “great blessing” which had come to South Carolina. Secessionists, and many subsequent history scholars, portrayed this historic moment as unanimous and inevitable.[^1] However, Charleston’s most respected lawyer and citizen did not join in the celebration. Although he kept himself apart from the prevailing disunion politics, he remained in South Carolina and continued to speak against Secessionism until the end of his life in 1862.[^2]

His name was James Louis Petigru and he is, perhaps, South Carolina’s most famous Unionist.

Petigru is often remembered for his quip about his home state: that South Carolina was “too small for a republic and too large for an insane asylum.” Although the exact delivery has since been called into question, it summarizes his attitude toward what he saw as South Carolina’s insanity in choosing to leave the Union.[^3] His entire adult life was spent fighting against the rising and growing tide of disunion perpetuated by the state’s fire eaters, nullifiers and secessionists. Petigru’s dissenting voice presents the often-overlooked historical truth that political conflict existed within South Carolina even as it marched toward secession.

Sometimes the idea of antebellum unionism is linked with abolition, Northern sympathies or political radicalism when it is written about by historians examining Unionism across the entire South. However, upon closer examination, South Carolina Unionists were usually conservative, legally minded and deeply loyal to their southern land and society. Most were not morally opposed to the South’s system of slavery and were very often enslavers themselves. While emotionally and ideologically these men remained loyal to the South, they disagreed fundamentally with their fellow South Carolinians’ belief that states had the right to ignore federal law or legally remove themselves from the Union.

Petigru’s life displays this resistance and the consequences of it.

Although he was born in what was considered the “backcountry” of South Carolina, James Louis Pettigrew (later Petigru as a demonstration of his allegiance to his mother’s Huguenot ancestry) quickly showed his intellectual capabilities and preference for academics over agriculture. He graduated at the top of his class from South Carolina College and taught school in Beaufort while preparing for his bar exam. During this period as a young adult, Petigru prioritized building and deepened his relationships with several lowcountry planters and their families. Among these friendships were William Drayton, Daniel Elliot Huger, and Hugh Legarè all of whom would play central roles as Unionists during the Nullification Crisis.[^4]

After passing the bar, Petigru built his law practice in Charleston and quickly became known locally and nationally for his intellect, wit and courtroom skill. As the Nullification Crisis unfolded in South Carolina and John C. Calhoun’s states’ rights theories began to hold sway over its citizens, Petigru the dissenter emerged. While his background did not fundamentally differ from his peers who sided with the states' rights party, his social network of Union loyalists may have determined his choice. His opposition to nullification, and later succession, became the defining feature of his social and political life.

For Petigru, the United States Constitution was the only safeguard against disorder and anarchy. The Union, governed by a federal government as outlined in the Constitution, provided the framework for the legal protection of Southern, and all American, rights. The only threat Petigru perceived was the dissolution of the Union. For Petigru, nullification was not only reckless, it was unlawful.

The legal basis of Petigru’s Unionism is an important key to understanding his political allegiances. He was not making an emotional or sentimental argument about Revolutionary era ideals. Nor was he primarily interested in preserving America as a symbol of republicanism for the rest of the world. Instead, he was making a constitutional argument based on his interpretation of the legal agreement between the states and the federal government. Southern interests could only be protected through operating within and under the laws and politics of the Union, not by separating themselves from it. 

Other insights into his choice to become and remain a Unionist are debated by historians. An incident which occurred between Petigru and a political opponent in which Petigru was driven to physical violence against a man who called him a “damned Federalist” gives historians clues as to the origins of his Unionism. When questioned about it later, he said he didn’t mind the other insults but that being called a Federalist was too close to the truth for him to not respond. Other historians argue that Petigru’s Unionism instead stemmed from his conviction that the federal government was legally granted legitimate authority over the states by the Constitution of the United States. It's certainly possibly that all of these factors helped to create and sustain Petigru's politics throughout his life.[^5]

But Petigru's constitutional interpretations and his unwillingness to yield his position, let him in an uncomfortable minority position.

While some elite white Unionists chose to leave the state after the Nullification Crisis of 1832-33 (a confrontation over federal tariff authority) fizzled out with the compromise orchestrated by Henry Clay, Daniel Webster and John C. Calhoun (sometimes called the “Great Triumvirate”), Petigru remained in Charleston. The result of his Unionism made it so that he was not able to regain formal political status until President Zachary Taylor’s election in 1848 when he accepted the presidential appointment as the Attorney General of South Carolina.[^6] During his time outside of formal political office, he continued to speak openly about his dissenting views on disunion and states' rights. He also continued to do business and socialize with his political adversaries. His dissent was not glaring or loud, but it was persistent and public.[^7]

In a broader sense, Unionism in antebellum South Carolina often mirrored Petigru’s personal life: a conservative, principled refusal to bow to the majority’s will. Petigru was highly legally trained, constitutionally focused, socially well-established and intellectually independent. Because of his reasoned loyalty to the Constitution, secession, for Petigru, was not a logical progression of Southern political thought. It was a rupture.

Petigru’s story complicates the oversimplification of South Carolina’s antebellum history. Even the first state to secede did not march in lock step toward disunion. There were many dissenting voices that steadily warned its citizens of the peril of removing themselves from the Union in the nearly half a century leading up to the Civil War.[^8] While Unionists were unable to ultimately stem the tide of secession in South Carolina, they did preserve an alternative vision of Southern constitutionalism which would become central to the Reconstruction debates and postwar legal culture. Petigru’s refusal to yield to secessionist ideology, and also his refusal to abandon his home state, embody his belief that loyalty and liberty were best protected together.
